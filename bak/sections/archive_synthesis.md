The archived notes converge on a small set of reusable claims for the StableBoundaryLayerGSPT report pipeline.

## Core Reduced Geometry

The minimal fast-slow model keeps $e$ as the fast turbulent variable and treats $(S, T_s, \Gamma)$ as slow. The regularized closure remains algebraic in $\sqrt{e+\delta}$, which makes the diffusivities strictly positive and provides a background-mixing floor.

## Transition Structure

The archive clarifies an important distinction: the reduced fast subsystem is naturally interpreted as a transcritical exchange in the regularized closure, while the broader coupled atmosphere-surface system can still exhibit fold-like or hysteretic behavior once the surface energy budget closes the feedback loop.

The blow-up method remains the correct technical tool for understanding the local transition geometry near non-hyperbolic points, especially when a delayed jump or canard-like passage needs to be resolved analytically rather than numerically.

## Surface Feedback and Hysteresis

The surface energy budget is the mechanism that makes the nocturnal system genuinely nonlinear. Radiative cooling strengthens inversion, inversion alters fluxes, and the fluxes feed back on both turbulence and temperature. That feedback is what supports brittle collapse, recovery, and hysteresis in the report narrative.

## Future Rotational Extension

The Coriolis-coupled vector-momentum extension is the clean path to inertial oscillations and Blackadar-style hodographs. The archive notes support treating this as a formal extension of the reference theory, not a replacement of the core scalar formulation.

## Geometric Language

Where useful, differential forms and the Hodge star provide a clean way to talk about the rotating-planet geometry of the atmospheric equations. That language is best kept as a supporting geometric layer for future manuscript drafts rather than a distraction from the core SBL closure.
