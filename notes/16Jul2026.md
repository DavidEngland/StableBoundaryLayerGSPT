## 3.1 Fast-Slow Scaling and Governing Equations

We define the state vector of the SCM as $\mathbf{X} = (U, V, T_s, e)^T$, where $U$ and $V$ represent the bulk horizontal wind components, $T_s$ is the land surface temperature, and $e \ge -\delta$ represents the TKE shifting above its background floor. We introduce the fast timescale $\tau = t / \varepsilon$, where $0 < \varepsilon \ll 1$ is the singular perturbation parameter representing the rapid adjustment time of subgrid-scale turbulence relative to the slow radiative and Coriolis forces.

The full singularly perturbed system is formulated as:

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{dU}{dt} &= f(V - V_g) - C_D \frac{\sqrt{U^2 + V^2}}{h} U \Phi_m(e), \\ \frac{dV}{dt} &= -f(U - U_g) - C_D \frac{\sqrt{U^2 + V^2}}{h} V \Phi_m(e), \\ \frac{dT_s}{dt} &= \frac{1}{C_g} \left[ R_n^{\downarrow} - \sigma T_s^4 - \rho c_p C_H \sqrt{U^2 + V^2} (T_s - T_a) \Phi_h(e) \right], \\ \varepsilon \frac{de}{dt} &= l_0 \left[ \eta \gamma (U^2 + V^2) - K \, G(T_s) \right] \Phi_m(e) - (e + \delta), \end{aligned}$$

where $h$ is the boundary layer depth, $C_g$ is the soil heat capacity, $V_g$ and $U_g$ are geostrophic wind components, and $K \in [\text{m s}^{-2}]$ is the kinematic buoyant acceleration scale. The background minimum TKE floor is represented by $\delta > 0$, ensuring the fast subsystem possesses a physical boundary limit at $e = -\delta$.

\section{Numerical Discretization and Singularity Safeguards}
\label{sec:numerical_discretization}

Numerical integration of the fast-slow system in Equation~\eqref{eq:mathematical_full_system} presents significant computational challenges. Due to the small singular perturbation parameter ($0 < \varepsilon \ll 1$), the system is highly stiff. Additionally, the classical mixing-length closure models the turbulent transport and dissipation terms using square-root dependencies on the shifted kinetic energy, $K_m, K_h \propto \sqrt{e+\delta}$.

If a standard numerical solver overshoots during a rapid collapse event, the state can be driven below the background threshold ($e \le -\delta$). This triggers a square-root singularity in the complex domain ($\mathbb{C}$), causing immediate solver failure or NaN propagation. To ensure physical forward invariance ($\mathcal{X}$ remaining in $\mathbb{R}_+$ for $e+\delta$), we must implement a smooth, state-dependent numerical gate.

\subsection{Mathematical Analysis of the Square-Root Singularity}
\label{subsec:singularity_analysis}

Let $\varphi \coloneqq e + \delta$ represent the shifted TKE coordinate. The fast subsystem in Equation~\eqref{eq:fast_subsystem} can be written in terms of $\varphi$ as:
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon \frac{d\varphi}{dt} = \sqrt{\varphi} , \Delta(\mathbf{y}) - \frac{\varphi^{3/2}}{l_0} = \sqrt{\varphi} \left( \Delta(\mathbf{y}) - \frac{\varphi}{l_0} \right),
\label{eq:shifted_fast_ode}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y} = (U, V, T_s)^T$ is the slow state vector, and $\Delta(\mathbf{y})$ is the net macro-environmental forcing balance.

The Jacobian of this fast subsystem with respect to the shifted fast coordinate is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \varphi} \left( \varepsilon \frac{d\varphi}{dt} \right) = \frac{\Delta(\mathbf{y})}{2\sqrt{\varphi}} - \frac{3\sqrt{\varphi}}{2l_0}.
\label{eq:fast_jacobian}
\end{equation}

As the boundary layer collapses and turbulence approaches the background floor ($\varphi \to 0^+$), the derivative exhibits a singular limit:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{\varphi \to 0^+} \frac{\partial}{\partial \varphi} \left( \varepsilon \frac{d\varphi}{dt} \right) =
\begin{cases}
+\infty & \text{if } \Delta(\mathbf{y}) > 0, \
-\infty & \text{if } \Delta(\mathbf{y}) < 0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:jacobian_singularity}
\end{equation}

This singularity causes extreme stiffness. In a discrete setting with step size $\Delta t$, any standard implicit or explicit scheme that evaluates a trial step where $\varphi_{k+1} < 0$ will crash. Truncating the state using a discontinuous projection (e.g., $\varphi = \max(0, \varphi)$) violates the differentiability requirements of modern adaptive-step and implicit Newton-based solvers, leading to step-rejection loops and stalling.

\subsection{The Smooth Safeguard Floor Gate}
\label{subsec:smooth_safeguard_gate}

To regularize the singularity while preserving $C^\infty$ smoothness, we introduce a state-dependent **safeguard floor gate** $\Psi(\varphi; \alpha)$ to the fast ODE. This gate smoothly forces the fast tendency to zero as $\varphi$ approaches a designated positive buffer threshold above the physical singularity.

We define the gate using a smooth, non-convex activation function:
\begin{equation}
\Psi(\varphi; \alpha) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ 1 + \tanh\left( \frac{\varphi - \alpha}{\alpha} \right) \right],
\label{eq:smooth_gate}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha > 0$ is a small, user-defined numerical safety scale (typically chosen such that $\alpha \sim 10^{-2} \delta$).

The gated fast subsystem is subsequently written as:
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon \frac{de}{dt} = \Psi(e+\delta; \alpha) \left[ \sqrt{e + \delta} \cdot \Delta(U, V, T_s) \right] - \frac{(e+\delta)^{3/2}}{l_0}.
\label{eq:gated_fast_subsystem}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Properties of the Safeguard Gate:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Asymptotic Consistency:} For active turbulent states where $(e+\delta) \gg \alpha$, the gate approaches unity exponentially fast:
\begin{equation}
\Psi(e+\delta; \alpha) = 1 - \mathcal{O}\left( e^{-2(e+\delta)/\alpha} \right),
\end{equation}
recovering the exact physical GSPT dynamics.
\item \textbf{Singularity Interception:} As the system collapses toward the laminar floor ($e \to -\delta$), the shifted variable $\varphi \to 0$. Since $\alpha > 0$, the gate term $\Psi$ rapidly vanishes. This smooth damping suppresses the positive destabilizing feedback of the buoyancy term before the square-root term's derivative can diverge.
\item \textbf{Forward Invariance of the Physical Domain:} Under this formulation, the boundary $e = -\delta$ becomes a smooth, weakly attracting invariant manifold of the numerical system, guaranteeing that $e(t) > -\delta$ for all $t > 0$ under any consistent discretization.
\end{enumerate}

    U  = x[2]
    V  = x[3]
    Ts = x[4]

    Delta      = shear_prod - buoy_dest

    return nothing

By replacing the traditional $C^0$ clipping operators ($\max(0, e)$) with the smooth hyperbolic embedding $e^*_{\xi}$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:smooth_embedding} and the activation gate $\Psi(e + \delta; \alpha)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:smooth_gate_formula}, the right-hand sides of our ODE system remain $C^\infty$ differentiable. Consequently, the analytical Jacobian:
\begin{equation}
J = \begin{pmatrix}
\frac{\partial \dot{e}}{\partial e} & \frac{\partial \dot{e}}{\partial U} & \frac{\partial \dot{e}}{\partial V} & \frac{\partial \dot{e}}{\partial T_s} \\
\frac{\partial \dot{U}}{\partial e} & \frac{\partial \dot{U}}{\partial U} & \frac{\partial \dot{U}}{\partial V} & 0 \\

\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
is defined continuously over the entire physical and non-physical state space. In the context of stable boundary layer (SBL) meteorology, **Geometric Singular Perturbation Theory (GSPT)** represents a fundamental paradigm shift away from **Monin-Obukhov Similarity Theory (MOST)**. While MOST has served as the standard empirical framework for decades, GSPT addresses its systemic failures in strongly stable conditions by reinterpreting the atmosphere as a dynamic, geometric manifold.

The relationship between these two theories can be understood through the following key distinctions:

### 1. Geometric Misprojection and the "Richardson Number"
The primary reason MOST fails in very stable conditions (vSBL) is that it attempts to project a **multi-dimensional, folded S-shaped manifold** onto a single, flat coordinate axis—the local gradient **Richardson number ($Ri$)**. MOST assumes a **single-valued relationship** between gradients and fluxes via empirical stability functions. GSPT reveals that the SBL is actually **multivalued and bistable**, meaning a single $Ri$ value cannot uniquely predict the state of the system during regime transitions.

### 2. Breakdown of the Quasi-Steady Assumption
MOST is rooted in the assumption that the SBL exists in a state of **local, steady-state equilibrium**, where the adjustment of turbulence is infinitely fast relative to macro-environmental forcing.
*   **MOST Assumption:** $\varepsilon \to 0$ instantaneously, meaning the fast variable (Turbulent Kinetic Energy) is always in equilibrium.
*   **GSPT Reality:** The system acknowledges a **finite singular perturbation parameter ($\varepsilon = 0.01$)**, representing the gap between rapid turbulent allocation and slow thermodynamic forcing. GSPT accounts for the **finite time-lag** and non-equilibrium physics that dictate the "brittle" collapse of turbulence, which MOST ignores.

### 3. Regime Applicability: wSBL vs. vSBL
The sources indicate that MOST remains a valid approximation for the **Weakly Stable Boundary Layer (wSBL)**, where turbulence is continuous and stratification is weak. However, in the **Very Stable Boundary Layer (vSBL)**, where radiative cooling dominates and turbulence becomes intermittent or collapses, MOST famously breaks down. GSPT defines exactly where the single-valued MOST assumption is valid (on the upper attracting turbulent branch) and provides a **regularized alternative** when the system "drops off the geometric cliff" into a collapsed state.

### 4. Mathematical Singularities and "Long-Tailed" Patches
Traditional models often use MOST-based "long-tailed" stability functions or arbitrary **minimum diffusivity floors** ($K_{\min} \approx 1 \text{ m}$) to prevent numerical "freezing" or runaway surface cooling.
*   In MOST, these are **ad-hoc empirical patches** required to keep solvers from crashing due to division-by-zero errors as $Ri$ becomes large.
*   In GSPT, a **strictly positive background mixing parameter ($\delta$)** arises naturally from the **invariant manifold architecture**. This provides a rigorous mathematical justification for residual mixing agents (like gravity waves) that MOST cannot represent.

### 5. From Empirical Fits to Geometric Flux Closures
GSPT replaces the empirical curves of MOST with a **regularized geometric flux closure**. Instead of tuning stability functions ($\phi_m, \phi_h$), the GSPT framework couples eddy diffusivities directly to the **manifold-constrained TKE coordinate ($e^*$)**. This ensures that the system transitions smoothly between regimes via a **transversal boundary crossing** or a **fold catastrophe**, maintaining $C^\infty$ differentiability for modern numerical solvers.
