When a vector field loses Lipschitz continuity, both the underlying **geometric manifold theory** and the **numerical integration algorithms** break down simultaneously—and for the exact same underlying reason: **the unbounded growth of the local Jacobian matrix.**

---

### 1. Why Non-Lipschitz Breaks Manifold Persistence (The Theory)

In Geometric Singular Perturbation Theory (GSPT), **Fenichel’s Persistence Theorems** state that if a critical manifold $\mathcal{M}_0$ is **normally hyperbolic** (meaning the fast eigenvalues off the manifold have non-zero real parts that strictly dominate the slow flow rates), it smoothly persists as an invariant manifold $\mathcal{M}_\epsilon$ for small $\epsilon > 0$.

However, Fenichel theory explicitly requires the vector field $f(x, y, \epsilon)$ to be at least $C^1$ smooth (and thus locally Lipschitz continuous).

* **Loss of Hyperbolicity / Spectral Gap:** Fenichel’s Hadamard graph-transform proofs rely on bounded spectral gaps. If $f$ is non-Lipschitz (e.g., $f(e) \propto \sqrt{e}$ near $e=0$), the derivative $\frac{\partial f}{\partial e} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{e}} \to \infty$ as $e \to 0$.
* **Collapse of the Invariant Sheet:** As the derivative diverges, the fast scale speed becomes infinite relative to the slow scale, violating the uniform bounds needed for normal hyperbolicity. The invariant manifold loses its smooth tangent bundle, leading to non-unique trajectory continuations or non-smooth "boundary corners" where fast-slow decomposition fails.

---

### 2. Why Non-Lipschitz Causes Severe Numerical Stiffness (The Computation)

Numerical ODE stiffness is dictated by the spectral radius of the local Jacobian matrix $J = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$:

$$\rho(J) = \max_i \vert{}\lambda_i\vert{}$$

When a system approaches a non-Lipschitz boundary (such as $e \to 0$ in TKE closures where turbulent velocity scale $q = \sqrt{e}$):

1. **Unbounded Jacobian:** Because $\frac{\partial \sqrt{e}}{\partial e} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{e}}$, the local Lipschitz constant $L \approx \Vert{}J\Vert{} \to \infty$.
2. **Step-Size Collapse in Explicit Solvers:** Explicit integrators (like Runge-Kutta) require step size $\Delta t < \frac{2}{\rho(J)}$. As $\rho(J) \to \infty$, the adaptive time-stepper forces $\Delta t \to 0$, stalling the simulation completely.
3. **Newton-Raphson Failure in Implicit Solvers:** Even fully implicit solvers (e.g., Backward Euler or TR-BDF2) fail. Implicit methods rely on local linear approximations to solve nonlinear residuals via Newton-Raphson iterations:

$$J \cdot \Delta x = -R$$



When $J$ changes infinitely fast near $e=0$, the linear approximation fails in every sub-step, causing the solver to exceed maximum convergence iterations and abort.

### The SBL Example: TKE Collapse at $e = 0$

In stable boundary layer models, eddy viscosity $K_m$ is governed by local TKE $e$:

$$K_m = c_k \, l \, \sqrt{e}$$

* **Without Regularization ($\sqrt{e}$):**
Near TKE collapse ($e \to 0$), the physical system attempts to go laminar. The non-Lipschitz point at $e = 0$ acts like an "infinite wall" for the numerical solver:
* **Theory:** $\mathcal{M}_0$ develops an infinitely steep tangent; Fenichel persistence fails.
* **Numerics:** $J \to \infty$; the time-stepper crashes with stiffness error or negative TKE grid shocks.


* **With Smooth Regularization ($\sqrt{e + \delta}$ or $\sqrt{e^2 + \delta^2}$):**
By inserting a tiny regularizer $\delta \approx 10^{-6}$:
* The derivative bound becomes $\frac{\partial q}{\partial e} \le \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\delta}} < \infty$.
* **Theory:** Local Lipschitz continuity is restored, keeping the manifold $C^1$-smooth everywhere.
* **Numerics:** The Jacobian stays strictly bounded, capping stiffness and allowing adaptive integrators to step smoothly through the laminar transition boundary.
