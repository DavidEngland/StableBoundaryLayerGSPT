To incorporate non-local transport or mass-flux (MF) schemes into your GSPT formulation without destroying its $C^\infty$ differentiability, we must target the mathematical "cliffs" typical of these schemes.

In traditional boundary layer models, the transitions between stable and unstable regimes are riddled with non-differentiable operators:

1. **Heaviside step functions** (e.g., turning on mass flux *only if* the surface buoyancy flux $B_s > 0$).
2. **Hard clamps** like $\max(0, B_s)$ to prevent taking roots of negative numbers.
3. **Non-differentiable power laws** like the convective velocity scale $w_* \propto B_s^{1/3}$, which has an infinite derivative ($d w_*/d B_s \to \infty$) at the neutral boundary $B_s = 0$.

If any of these enter your system, Fenichel's theorem no longer applies because the critical manifold is torn or ceases to be smooth. To preserve the smooth geometry of the slow manifold, we can regularize these transitions using a **smooth partition of unity** and **hyperbolic scaling regularizers**.

## 1. Hyperbolic Regularization of the Convective Scale ($w_*$)

The classic convective velocity scale is defined as:


$$w_* = \left( \frac{g}{\theta_0} \overline{w'\theta'}_s z_i \right)^{1/3}$$

where $B_s = \frac{g}{\theta_0} \overline{w'\theta'}_s z_i$ is the surface buoyancy flux, and $z_i$ is the boundary layer height. To make this $C^\infty$ smooth across the stable-to-unstable transition ($B_s = 0$), we introduce a small regularization parameter $\delta_w > 0$ and define a smooth convective scale $w_{*, \delta}$:

$$w_{*, \delta}(B_s) = \left( \frac{B_s + \sqrt{B_s^2 + \delta_w}}{2} \right)^{1/3}$$

### Why this works topologically:

* **When $B_s \gg 0$ (strongly convective):** $w_{*, \delta} \approx B_s^{1/3}$, recovering the exact physical limit.
* **When $B_s \ll 0$ (stable):** The term inside the parenthesis smoothly asymptotes to $0$ without ever hitting a hard zero or becoming negative.
* **At $B_s = 0$ (neutral):** The term inside is strictly positive ($\sqrt{\delta_w} / 2$), meaning the cube root is infinitely differentiable ($C^\infty$) everywhere, completely bypassing the $x^{1/3}$ derivative singularity.

---

## 2. A $C^\infty$ Blending Operator for Non-Local Fluxes

Whether you choose a counter-gradient term ($\gamma$) or an active mass-flux term ($M(\theta_u - \bar{\theta})$), you only want it active during unstable conditions. We can construct an algebraic sigmoid transition function $\sigma_\epsilon(B_s)$ that acts as a smooth switch:

$$\sigma_\epsilon(B_s) = \frac{1}{2} \left( 1 + \frac{B_s}{\sqrt{B_s^2 + \epsilon}} \right)$$

where $\epsilon > 0$ is a transition-width parameter.

* When $B_s < 0$ (stable), $\sigma_\epsilon \to 0$.
* When $B_s > 0$ (unstable), $\sigma_\epsilon \to 1$.
* The transition at $B_s = 0$ is perfectly smooth.

Here, the mass flux $M_{\delta}$ is driven by our regularized convective scale:
